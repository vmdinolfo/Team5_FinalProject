History of Stroke



Strokes, originally known as apoplexy, were discovered by the Greek physician Hippocrates over 2400 years ago. Greeks defined apoplexy as “struck down by violence.” This was because someone who suffered from apoplexy suddenly developed paralysis and change in demeanor.

It was not until the mid-1600s that Jacob Wepfer found there was bleeding in the brain in those who suffered and died from apoplexy. Eventually in 1928, apoplexy was divided into two categories: Ischemic and Hemorrhagic.

Ischemic stroke occurs when blood clots lodge in the brain and is typically the most common type of stroke. Hemorrhagic stroke occurs when blood vessels in the brain break open. Stroke continues to be a leading cause of disability, however, 80% of strokes are preventable. There are five known risk factors:

  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Congestive heart failure
  • Diabetes
  • High blood pressure
  • A history of stroke or transient ischemic attack
The American Stroke Association has created an acronym to quickly spot stroke signs: F.A.S.T.

F: Face Drooping
A: Arm Weakness
S: Speech Difficulty
T: Time to Call 911
